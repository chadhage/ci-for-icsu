# A3 Thinking

## TL;DR

A3 — named after the A3-size paper (~11"×17") it fits on — is a structured one-page report that captures the entire arc of a problem-solving effort: background, current state, goal, root-cause analysis, countermeasures, plan, follow-up. Developed at Toyota, the A3 is both a *thinking tool* and a *coaching tool*: the constraint of one page forces clarity, and the act of drafting an A3 with a mentor (the "A3 coach") teaches structured reasoning. For CSAs, A3 is the right artifact when you want a single, durable, communicable record of a Belt project, a Kaizen, or any DMAIC cycle — and when you want to grow customer engineers' analytical skill by coaching them through one.

## Why A3

Most improvement work is documented in 40-slide decks nobody re-reads. A3 inverts that — one page that captures the entire thinking, readable in 5 minutes, durable for years.

- Forces sharp problem statements — there is no room for fluff.
- Shows the *reasoning*, not just the outcome — future readers learn the why.
- Standardizes the shape of problem-solving across the org.
- Doubles as a coaching mechanism: A3 author + coach + iterative review = belt-grade training.

**Real example:** A customer's platform team produced 90-slide quarterly reviews nobody read. The CSA replaced them with one A3 per initiative. Leadership reading time fell from 2 hours to 15 minutes; decision quality improved (measured by reversal rate of those decisions one quarter later — from 40% to 12%). One page beat ninety.

## What A3 is

Standard A3 layout (left to right, top to bottom):

| Left Side                              | Right Side                                  |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Background / context                | 5. Countermeasures (what to do)             |
| 2. Current state (with data/diagram)   | 6. Implementation plan (who, what, when)    |
| 3. Goal / target state                 | 7. Follow-up (how to verify, sustain)       |
| 4. Root cause analysis                 |                                             |

Variants:

- **Problem-solving A3** — the canonical structure above.
- **Proposal A3** — for proposing new initiatives (Status → Background → Recommendation → Details → Plan).
- **Status A3** — for tracking ongoing work (Plan → Actuals → Variances → Next steps).
- **Strategy A3** — paired with [Hoshin Kanri](./hoshin-kanri.md) at higher levels.

Discipline:

- One page. Two if absolutely required; never three.
- Data and visuals dominate; prose is minimal.
- The story must read left-to-right, top-to-bottom.
- Author signs, coach signs, sponsor signs.

## How to use A3

1. **Pick the problem.** Pair with a coach. Sketch the A3 by hand first; tooling later.
2. **Draft Background and Current State.** Include the baseline data. If you can't get data, that's your first finding.
3. **Set the Target.** Quantified, dated, owned.
4. **Root cause.** Use [Ishikawa](./ishikawa.md), [5 Whys](./5-whys.md), [Pareto](./pareto-chart.md) on the page itself.
5. **Countermeasures.** Tie each to a root cause. No orphan actions.
6. **Plan.** Who / what / when. Brief.
7. **Follow-up.** How will we verify the gain held? What's the check date?
8. **Review with coach, then sponsor.** Iterate. Final A3 goes into the knowledge base, not a shared drive nobody opens.

**Anti-pattern to avoid:** A3 as a slide. If your A3 fits a slide deck, it's not an A3 — it's a status report wearing the label.

## When to use A3

- Wrapping a DMAIC project as a permanent artifact.
- Documenting a Kaizen event readout.
- Belt certification deliverable.
- Coaching new Green Belts.
- Proposing a new program (proposal A3).
- Capturing a postmortem deeper than a runbook entry.

Do **not** use A3 when:

- A 2-line runbook entry would do.
- The audience is broad, asynchronous, and unfamiliar (write a doc instead).

## Where A3 shows up

- Belt certification portfolios.
- Kaizen readouts.
- Customer engagement summary archives.
- iCSU CoP playbook contributions (see [ms-ci-cop.md](./ms-ci-cop.md)).
- Coaching sessions (A3 + coach is the practice).

## Who uses A3

- **Belt candidates** — primary authors.
- **Belt coaches / CSA** — review and challenge.
- **Sponsors** — sign off; archive.
- **Process owners** — receive A3 + standard work bundle.
- **CoP librarians** — curate A3 archive as searchable knowledge base.

## Examples

### Example 1 — Cosmos cost A3

Background: chat-history $11.4K/mo. Current: P95 38K RU/s, 412 429s/day. Target: <$3K/mo, 0 429s. Root cause (5W on Ishikawa): IaC predated HPK GA. Countermeasures: HPK + autoscale + index trim + SDK upgrade. Plan: 5-day Kaizen. Follow-up: monthly dashboard, IaC module promoted.

### Example 2 — AKS reliability A3

ImagePullBackOff signature elimination summarized on one page that has been re-used across 4 adjacent customers verbatim.

### Example 3 — Belt project A3

Customer engineer's Green Belt deliverable. One A3 + sustainment plan; certified.

### Example 4 — Postmortem A3 vs. runbook entry

Routine incidents → runbook entry. Repeated / systemic incidents → A3 with root-cause analysis. Customer SRE org adopted the split; learning velocity rose.

### Example 5 — Proposal A3 instead of a deck

CSA proposed a $1.2M modernization program on a one-page proposal A3. Approved in the same meeting; the equivalent deck had been declined twice prior.

### Example 6 — Coaching cycle

Belt candidate redrafted A3 six times under coach guidance. The redrafts *were* the training; the final A3 is incidental.

### Example 7 — Strategy A3 (Hoshin pair)

Top-level A3 stated 3 annual breakthrough objectives. Cascaded into 12 sub-A3s. Visible alignment across 4 product groups for the first time.

### Example 8 — A3 archive as knowledge base

Customer accumulated 67 A3s across 18 months. Search across them seeded 3 new improvement projects from past learnings.

### Example 9 — Failed project A3

A3 documented an abandoned initiative with the disconfirming evidence. Saved the next team 6 weeks of duplicate exploration.

### Example 10 — A3 as engagement summary

CSA replaced quarterly customer status decks with one A3 per workstream. Average customer-leadership review time halved; sponsor decision quality measurably improved.
